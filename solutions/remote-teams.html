Remote work created a context gap that chat messages can't fill.

Distributed teams have solved the logistics of remote work — the video calls, the project boards, the async check-ins. But they haven't solved the context problem. When a conversation happens in a meeting that only half the team could attend, the other half gets a two-sentence Slack summary. When decisions are made across timezones, the reasoning behind them evaporates by the time the other hemisphere wakes up.

The result is a slow accumulation of misalignment. People make assumptions about what was decided. They miss the nuance of why something was prioritized. They feel disconnected not because they lack tools, but because they lack context — the rich, conversational context that used to happen naturally when everyone was in the same office.

Remote teams don't need more meetings. They need better access to the meetings that already happen.

A distributed team that stopped losing context across timezones

A sixty-person SaaS company with engineers in Europe, designers in North America, and a customer success team in Southeast Asia had a persistent alignment problem. Key decisions were made in meetings that only one timezone could attend. By the time everyone caught up, the context had been filtered through summaries and secondhand interpretations.

The company gave each team member a Momentum Personal device and integrated the platform with their Slack workspace and Notion knowledge base. The change was structural. Every meeting — from daily standups to architecture reviews to customer feedback sessions — produced a searchable transcript and a concise summary that was automatically posted to the relevant Slack channel.

Engineers in Berlin could review the exact product discussion that happened in a San Francisco afternoon meeting before their own morning standup. The customer success team in Manila could search across weeks of product meetings to find when a specific feature decision was made and why.

Within two months, the company reported a significant reduction in "re-explanation meetings" — those follow-up calls that existed solely to bring people up to speed. Team members described feeling more connected to the company's direction despite being spread across twelve timezones.

"We used to have this painful pattern where every meeting started with fifteen minutes of re-explaining what happened in the last meeting for the people who couldn't attend. Now everyone reads the transcript before the call and we start from a shared understanding. We've essentially added hours of productive time back into every week across the entire company."

David Park, VP of Engineering, Distributed SaaS Company
